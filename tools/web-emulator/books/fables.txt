# The Fox and the Grapes

A fox, dry-mouthed from a long morning of finding nothing, came upon a vine trained high over a farm wall. Fat bunches of grapes hung just under the coping, dusty purple and warm in the sun.

He jumped for them. He backed up along the lane and took a running jump for them. He tried the wall, and the trellis, and one undignified scramble up a water barrel that ended in the ditch.

At last he sat down in the road, looked up at the grapes a final time, and trotted off with his nose in the air.

"They were sour anyway," he said. "Probably full of wasps."

> It is easy to despise what you cannot have.

# The Tortoise and the Hare

A hare was making fun of a tortoise on the subject of speed, which was not a fair subject, and the tortoise knew it. "All the same," said the tortoise, "I will race you to the old gate, and I will win."

The hare laughed so hard he agreed to it. At the start he was out of sight in three heartbeats, and by the time he reached the half-way meadow the whole idea struck him as so absurd that he lay down in the clover to let the tortoise catch up a little. The sun was warm. The clover was deep.

The tortoise walked. He walked while the hare slept, and he walked while the hare dreamed of winning, and he was one step short of the gate when the hare opened his eyes.

The hare crossed the meadow faster than he had ever run in his life, and lost by a neck the length of a tortoise's neck.

> Steady going wins the race that talent takes a nap in.

# The North Wind and the Sun

The North Wind and the Sun fell to arguing about which of them was the stronger, and agreed to settle it on the first traveller to come up the road: whichever could get the man's cloak off him had won.

The Wind went first. He came down the valley with everything he had, tore leaves off the trees, bent the hedges flat, and howled around the traveller from every quarter at once. The man staggered, planted his feet, and wrapped the cloak around himself so tight he looked like a chrysalis. The harder the Wind blew, the tighter he held it.

Then the Wind sat down, out of breath, and the Sun came out quietly from behind a cloud. He warmed the stones of the road. He warmed the man's shoulders, and the back of his neck, and the whole green valley around him.

The traveller loosened the cloak. Half a mile on he unfastened it; at the ford he folded it over his arm, and sat down on the bank in his shirtsleeves to eat his bread in the sunshine.

> Gentleness carries the point that force cannot.

# The Lion and the Mouse

A mouse, hurrying home in the dark, ran straight across the nose of a sleeping lion. The paw came down before she could squeak.

"Let me go," said the mouse, "and one day I will repay you." The lion was so entertained by the idea of a mouse repaying a lion that he lifted the paw and let her run.

Not long after, the lion put his foot in a hunter's rope snare, and his roaring filled the whole forest. The mouse knew the voice, and came, and gnawed through the rope where it was thinnest, strand by strand, until the loop fell open.

"You laughed at me once," she said. "But it was not the size of the promise that mattered."

> No kindness is ever wasted, and no friend too small.
